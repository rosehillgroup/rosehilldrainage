Understanding Rural Grip Drains

Grip drains are shallow channels cut across roadside verges to redirect surface water into adjacent ditches. They are a key component of rural drainage, typically spaced every 10‚Äì30 metres along the verge of rural roads.

With approximately 313,500 miles of rural roads in the UK, and assuming an average of 1,000 grips per mile, there are potentially over 300 million individual grip drains across the national network. Local authorities are responsible for maintaining their regional networks.

A rural council with 100 miles (160 km) of classified roads may manage over 50,000 grip drains. Maintenance regimes vary, but in many cases, annual / biannual cleaning or inspection of rural grips and re-cutting or re-forming (i.e., re-trenching) typically occurs every 5‚Äì10 years, depending on silt buildup and erosion.

This is to restore function and prevent localised flooding and represents a significant ongoing cost and labour burden, often absorbing seasonal budgets.
